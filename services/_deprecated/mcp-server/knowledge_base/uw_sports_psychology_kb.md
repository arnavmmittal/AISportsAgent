# UW Sports Psychology AI Knowledge Base

## Part 1: Discovery-First Methodology & Mental Skills Framework

### Discovery-First Conversation Protocol

The Discovery-First methodology is the foundation of effective sports psychology support. Before offering any advice, framework, or intervention, we must first understand the athlete's unique situation, context, and needs.

#### The 6-Phase Conversation Structure

**Phase 1: CHECK-IN (Discovery)**
Purpose: Understand current state and context
- Open with genuine curiosity about their day/practice/competition
- Listen for emotional undertones and energy levels
- Identify what brought them to the conversation today
- Avoid assumptions about what they need

Key questions:
- "What's on your mind today?"
- "How have things been going since we last talked?"
- "What's been taking up most of your mental energy lately?"

**Phase 2: CLARIFY (Understanding)**
Purpose: Deepen understanding of the specific challenge
- Ask follow-up questions to understand the full picture
- Explore the context (when, where, with whom)
- Understand the impact on their performance and wellbeing
- Identify patterns or recurring themes

Key questions:
- "Can you tell me more about what that looks like for you?"
- "When do you notice this happening most?"
- "How is this affecting your training/competition?"

**Phase 3: FORMULATION (Synthesis)**
Purpose: Collaboratively make sense of what's happening
- Reflect back what you've heard
- Offer a tentative understanding (not diagnosis)
- Check if your understanding resonates
- Invite correction or clarification

Example: "So it sounds like when you're in high-pressure situations, you notice your thoughts racing and it becomes hard to focus on your game plan. Does that capture it?"

**Phase 4: INTERVENTION (Framework Application)**
Purpose: Introduce relevant mental skills or frameworks
- Only after understanding, suggest appropriate techniques
- Explain the why behind the intervention
- Adapt frameworks to their sport and situation
- Keep it practical and actionable

**Phase 5: PLAN (Action Steps)**
Purpose: Create concrete next steps
- Develop specific, achievable action items
- Identify when and how they'll practice new skills
- Anticipate obstacles and problem-solve
- Schedule follow-up if appropriate

**Phase 6: WRAP-UP (Closing)**
Purpose: End with clarity and connection
- Summarize key takeaways
- Affirm their effort and progress
- Express confidence in their ability
- Leave the door open for continued support


### Mental Skills Assessment Framework

#### Beginner Level Mental Skills

Athletes at the beginner level are building foundational awareness and basic self-regulation skills.

**Core Focus Areas:**
1. **Body Awareness** - Recognizing physical sensations and tension
2. **Emotional Identification** - Naming feelings (nervous, excited, frustrated)
3. **Basic Breathing** - Diaphragmatic breathing for calming
4. **Simple Self-Talk** - Identifying negative vs. positive thoughts
5. **Goal Understanding** - Distinguishing outcome vs. process goals

**Assessment Indicators (Beginner):**
- Can identify when feeling nervous vs. confident
- Knows basic breathing techniques but doesn't use consistently
- Has goals but they may be vague or outcome-focused
- Limited awareness of pre-competition routine importance
- May struggle to articulate what helps them perform well

**Recommended Interventions (Beginner):**
- Teach basic breathing techniques (box breathing, 4-7-8)
- Introduce body scan awareness
- Help create simple pre-competition routine
- Practice identifying helpful vs. unhelpful thoughts
- Set one clear process goal per week


#### Intermediate Level Mental Skills

Athletes at the intermediate level have basic awareness and are building consistency in applying mental skills.

**Core Focus Areas:**
1. **Arousal Regulation** - Adjusting energy up or down as needed
2. **Focus Control** - Directing and redirecting attention
3. **Constructive Self-Talk** - Actively using helpful internal dialogue
4. **Visualization Basics** - Creating simple mental images of success
5. **Performance Routines** - Consistent pre-shot/pre-play routines

**Assessment Indicators (Intermediate):**
- Uses breathing techniques in practice but inconsistent in competition
- Can identify optimal arousal zone but doesn't always reach it
- Has visualization practice but images may lack vividness
- Maintains focus for periods but loses it under pressure
- Has routines but may abandon them when stressed

**Recommended Interventions (Intermediate):**
- Develop personalized arousal regulation strategies
- Practice attention control exercises (single-point focus)
- Build detailed visualization scripts for key moments
- Create competition-specific mental preparation plan
- Introduce cue words for refocusing


#### Advanced Level Mental Skills

Athletes at the advanced level have strong awareness and can apply mental skills flexibly across situations.

**Core Focus Areas:**
1. **Flow State Access** - Recognizing and facilitating optimal performance states
2. **Adaptive Mindset** - Viewing challenges as growth opportunities
3. **Metacognition** - Awareness of one's own thinking patterns
4. **Performance Under Pressure** - Thriving in high-stakes moments
5. **Integrated Identity** - Healthy relationship with athletic role

**Assessment Indicators (Advanced):**
- Consistently enters optimal performance state in competition
- Views setbacks as learning opportunities without prompting
- Can analyze own mental game and make adjustments
- Maintains or elevates performance under pressure
- Has balanced identity beyond just being an athlete

**Recommended Interventions (Advanced):**
- Explore conditions that facilitate flow states
- Develop personal performance philosophy
- Practice advanced visualization (multi-sensory, scenario-based)
- Build resilience through intentional adversity training
- Cultivate meaning and purpose beyond performance outcomes


### Sport-Specific Mental Demands

#### Basketball Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Managing fast-paced decisions, handling crowd pressure, free throw consistency
- **Key Mental Skills:** Focus recovery after mistakes, confidence with ball, team communication
- **Pressure Moments:** Free throws, end of game situations, playing from behind
- **Unique Considerations:** Visible to crowd/cameras, individual stats tracked, bench dynamics

**Basketball-Specific Interventions:**
- Free throw routine development (consistent timing, breath, cue word)
- "Next play" mentality training for mistake recovery
- Visualization of successful end-game scenarios
- Managing individual stats pressure vs. team success

#### Soccer Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** 90-minute focus maintenance, penalty kick pressure, position-specific demands
- **Key Mental Skills:** Sustained concentration, composure under fatigue, tactical awareness
- **Pressure Moments:** Penalty kicks, final minutes of close games, defensive errors
- **Unique Considerations:** Limited substitutions, weather conditions, international competition

**Soccer-Specific Interventions:**
- Penalty kick routine and visualization
- Energy management across 90 minutes
- Refocusing techniques for in-game application
- Building resilience for scoring droughts

#### Swimming Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Pre-race anxiety, monotony of training, tenths of seconds determining outcomes
- **Key Mental Skills:** Race visualization, pain tolerance, taper psychology
- **Pressure Moments:** Behind the blocks, turns, final 25 meters
- **Unique Considerations:** Individual sport with team elements, time-based outcomes, sensory deprivation in water

**Swimming-Specific Interventions:**
- Detailed race visualization including all phases
- Pre-race routine behind the blocks
- Managing taper anxiety and rest psychology
- Pain acceptance strategies for training and racing

#### Track and Field Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Performance in single attempts, managing attempts/throws, peak performance timing
- **Key Mental Skills:** Consistent routine execution, one-shot mentality, long competition management
- **Pressure Moments:** Final attempts, qualifying rounds, weather delays
- **Unique Considerations:** Multiple events for some, long wait times between attempts, visible individual performance

**Track-Specific Interventions:**
- Approach/throw routine development
- Energy management during long competitions
- Visualization of personal best performances
- Coping with false starts/fouls

#### Volleyball Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Quick error recovery, serving pressure, momentum shifts
- **Key Mental Skills:** Short-term memory for mistakes, consistent serve routine, team energy management
- **Pressure Moments:** Serve receive, crucial serves, fifth set
- **Unique Considerations:** Rally scoring magnifies each play, rotation changes, visible body language

**Volleyball-Specific Interventions:**
- Serve routine (consistent toss, breath, target visualization)
- "Flush and focus" for error recovery
- Team energy reading and contribution
- Managing momentum swings

#### Football Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Position-specific pressures, high-contact aggression, playbook complexity
- **Key Mental Skills:** Controlled aggression, play execution under pressure, physical courage
- **Pressure Moments:** Key plays, fourth quarter, rivalry games
- **Unique Considerations:** High injury risk, specialized positions, extreme team hierarchy

**Football-Specific Interventions:**
- Play visualization (reading defense, execution)
- Arousal control for optimal aggression
- Managing fear/anxiety about contact
- Leadership and team role development

#### Tennis Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Isolation on court, long match management, self-coaching
- **Key Mental Skills:** Between-point routine, momentum management, self-talk control
- **Pressure Moments:** Break points, tie-breaks, match points
- **Unique Considerations:** No coaching during play, visible frustration consequences, individual accountability

**Tennis-Specific Interventions:**
- 16-second between-point routine
- Self-coaching strategies for problem-solving
- Managing negative body language
- Visualization of tie-break scenarios

#### Golf Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Managing four-hour focus, yips prevention, course management
- **Key Mental Skills:** Pre-shot routine, patience, present-moment focus
- **Pressure Moments:** First tee, short putts, final holes
- **Unique Considerations:** Slow pace allows overthinking, weather dependency, individual scoring with playing partners

**Golf-Specific Interventions:**
- Pre-shot routine development (complete script)
- Thought management between shots
- Visualization of course strategy
- Building trust in putting stroke

#### Baseball/Softball Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Long season mental endurance, slump management, sporadic action
- **Key Mental Skills:** At-bat routine, position-specific focus, seasonal perspective
- **Pressure Moments:** Full count situations, defensive pressure, playoffs
- **Unique Considerations:** Statistical nature invites overthinking, long season with many games, pitcher-hitter psychological battle

**Baseball/Softball-Specific Interventions:**
- At-bat routine and box ritual
- Slump-busting mental strategies
- Managing between-pitch time
- Pitcher-catcher mental communication

#### Gymnastics Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Fear of injury, scoring subjectivity, routine perfection
- **Key Mental Skills:** Fear management, routine visualization, composure under judgment
- **Pressure Moments:** Difficult skills, competition start, individual events
- **Unique Considerations:** Early career pressure, body image concerns, subjective scoring

**Gymnastics-Specific Interventions:**
- Fear reduction protocols for skills
- Complete routine visualization
- Managing judges' scores response
- Body image and identity work

#### Wrestling Mental Demands
- **Primary Challenges:** Weight management stress, one-on-one combat intensity, tournament bracketing
- **Key Mental Skills:** Weight cut psychology, aggressive confidence, match strategy
- **Pressure Moments:** First period aggression, overtime, rivalry matches
- **Unique Considerations:** Physical confrontation, weight cutting impact on mental state, individual accountability

**Wrestling-Specific Interventions:**
- Weight cut mental management
- Pre-match aggression building
- Visualization of technique execution
- Managing weight class expectations


## Part 2: Evidence-Based Frameworks & Visualization Training

### 8 Evidence-Based Mental Performance Frameworks

#### Framework 1: Brian Tracy's Goal-Setting Model

Brian Tracy's approach emphasizes written goals with specific deadlines and action plans.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Clarity** - Define exactly what you want to achieve
2. **Written Goals** - Writing activates more brain areas than just thinking
3. **Deadline Setting** - Creates urgency and focus
4. **Action Plans** - Break down into daily/weekly steps
5. **Daily Review** - Read goals morning and night

**Application for Athletes:**
- Write performance goals in present tense ("I compete with confidence")
- Set season, monthly, weekly, and daily training goals
- Create specific action items for each practice
- Review goals before training and competition
- Track progress in a performance journal

**Sample Script:**
"Let's use Brian Tracy's goal-setting approach. First, what do you want to achieve this season? Be as specific as possible. Now let's write it down in present tense as if you've already achieved it. What's the date by which you want to achieve this? Great. Now, what are three things you can do this week to move toward that goal?"


#### Framework 2: James Clear's Atomic Habits

James Clear's framework focuses on small, consistent habits that compound over time.

**Core Principles:**
1. **1% Better Every Day** - Focus on tiny improvements
2. **Habit Stacking** - Attach new habits to existing ones
3. **Environment Design** - Make good habits obvious and easy
4. **Identity-Based Habits** - Focus on who you want to become
5. **Systems Over Goals** - Focus on the process, not just outcomes

**Application for Athletes:**
- Stack mental skills practice with physical warm-up
- Design training environment to cue mental preparation
- Build identity as "someone who trains their mind"
- Focus on daily mental training process
- Make mental preparation the default, not an add-on

**Sample Script:**
"James Clear talks about making habits tiny and attaching them to existing routines. What's something you already do before every practice? Great. Can we attach one minute of visualization right after that? So the new pattern is: [existing habit] → 60 seconds of visualization. This week, just focus on doing it consistently. We'll build from there."


#### Framework 3: Dr. Nate Zinsser's Confident Mind

Dr. Nate Zinsser, director of West Point's performance psychology program, emphasizes building an unshakeable mental bank account of confidence.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Mental Bank Account** - Deposit positive memories, withdraw confidence
2. **Selective Attention** - Choose what you focus on and remember
3. **Constructive Memory** - Actively rehearse past successes
4. **Affirmation Practice** - Use first-person, present-tense statements
5. **Top Dog Mentality** - Act like you've already arrived

**Application for Athletes:**
- Create a "highlight reel" of best performances
- Before competition, make "deposits" by reviewing successes
- After good performances, actively process and store memories
- Develop personal affirmations tied to identity
- Practice walking and talking like a champion

**Sample Script:**
"Dr. Zinsser says confidence is like a bank account - you can make deposits and withdrawals. Let's make some deposits now. Tell me about a time you performed really well. What did that feel like? Let's hold onto that feeling. Now, tell me another time. And another. These are your deposits. Before your next competition, I want you to spend 5 minutes reviewing these moments. You're building wealth in your confidence account."


#### Framework 4: Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's Flow State

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's research identifies the conditions for optimal experience and peak performance.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Challenge-Skill Balance** - Tasks match abilities
2. **Clear Goals** - Know exactly what you're trying to do
3. **Immediate Feedback** - Know how you're doing moment-to-moment
4. **Deep Focus** - Complete concentration on the task
5. **Loss of Self-Consciousness** - Freed from worry about judgment

**Application for Athletes:**
- Set clear performance goals for each practice/game
- Design training with appropriate challenge level
- Create feedback loops during training
- Practice single-pointed focus exercises
- Use routines to quiet self-consciousness

**Sample Script:**
"Flow happens when challenge matches skill, you have clear goals, and you're getting immediate feedback. Let's design your next practice with this in mind. What skill do you want to work on? How can we make it challenging enough to engage you but not so hard you get frustrated? What feedback will tell you if you're doing it right? Now you've got conditions for flow."


#### Framework 5: Carol Dweck's Growth Mindset

Carol Dweck's research distinguishes between fixed and growth mindsets and their impact on learning and performance.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Effort > Talent** - Ability is developed through dedication
2. **Embrace Challenges** - Difficulties are opportunities to grow
3. **Learn from Criticism** - Feedback helps improvement
4. **Find Inspiration in Others' Success** - Not threatened by others doing well
5. **"Not Yet" Thinking** - Haven't mastered it yet, but will

**Application for Athletes:**
- Reframe failures as learning opportunities
- Praise effort and process, not just outcomes
- View successful competitors as inspiration
- Use "yet" language ("I haven't mastered this yet")
- Seek out challenges that stretch abilities

**Sample Script:**
"You mentioned you're 'not good at' pressure situations. Let's reframe that. What if we said you 'haven't mastered pressure yet'? That 'yet' is powerful. It means you're on a journey. Everyone who's great at pressure was once not good at it. What's one thing you could learn from your last pressure moment that could help you grow?"


#### Framework 6: Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) for Performance

ACT helps athletes develop psychological flexibility and values-driven action.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Acceptance** - Allow uncomfortable thoughts and feelings without fighting
2. **Cognitive Defusion** - See thoughts as mental events, not literal truths
3. **Present Moment** - Stay grounded in now, not past or future
4. **Self-as-Context** - You are more than your thoughts and feelings
5. **Values Clarification** - Know what truly matters to you
6. **Committed Action** - Take steps aligned with values despite discomfort

**Application for Athletes:**
- Practice accepting pre-competition nerves
- Defuse from unhelpful thoughts ("I'm having the thought that...")
- Use grounding techniques before competition
- Clarify athletic and personal values
- Commit to valued action even when anxious

**Sample Script:**
"Instead of fighting your nerves, what if we learned to make room for them? Try this: notice where the anxiety shows up in your body. Rather than pushing it away, can you breathe into that space and allow it to be there? The goal isn't to eliminate nerves - it's to compete well even with nerves present. Your values of [giving full effort/being a good teammate] are more important than comfort."


#### Framework 7: CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) for Sports

CBT helps athletes identify and change unhelpful thought patterns that impact performance.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Thoughts Influence Feelings and Behavior** - Cognitive triangle
2. **Identify Cognitive Distortions** - All-or-nothing, catastrophizing, etc.
3. **Challenge Unhelpful Thoughts** - Question automatic assumptions
4. **Replace with Balanced Thoughts** - More realistic and helpful
5. **Behavioral Experiments** - Test beliefs through action

**Application for Athletes:**
- Identify automatic thoughts before/during/after competition
- Recognize cognitive distortions common in athletes
- Develop thought challenging questions
- Create balanced performance self-talk
- Design experiments to test limiting beliefs

**Common Cognitive Distortions in Athletes:**
- **All-or-Nothing:** "If I don't win, I'm a failure"
- **Catastrophizing:** "If I miss this shot, we'll lose everything"
- **Mind Reading:** "My coach thinks I can't handle pressure"
- **Fortune Telling:** "I know I'm going to choke"
- **Discounting Positives:** "That was lucky, not skill"

**Sample Script:**
"Let's look at that thought: 'I always choke under pressure.' Is that literally true every single time? Can you think of one time you handled pressure well? So the more accurate thought might be: 'I sometimes struggle with pressure, but I've also performed well under pressure before.' How does that thought feel different?"


#### Framework 8: Mindfulness-Based Performance Enhancement

Mindfulness training develops present-moment awareness and non-judgmental attention.

**Core Principles:**
1. **Present-Moment Focus** - Attention on now, not past or future
2. **Non-Judgmental Awareness** - Observing without evaluation
3. **Attention Training** - Strengthening focus muscle
4. **Beginner's Mind** - Approaching each moment fresh
5. **Letting Go** - Releasing attachment to outcomes

**Application for Athletes:**
- Daily mindfulness meditation practice (even 5 minutes)
- Mindful movement during warm-up
- Breath awareness before competition
- Non-judgmental awareness of mistakes during competition
- Letting go of outcomes between points/plays

**Sample Script:**
"Mindfulness is about training your attention. Let's practice right now. Close your eyes and take three deep breaths. Now, without trying to change anything, just notice the sensations in your body. Where do you feel tension? Where do you feel calm? Just observe, without judging as good or bad. This is the foundation of competition focus - being fully present, moment to moment."


### PETTLEP Visualization Framework

PETTLEP is an evidence-based visualization model developed by Holmes and Collins (2001) that maximizes the effectiveness of mental imagery.

#### The 7 PETTLEP Components

**P - Physical**
- Adopt your actual physical position during imagery
- Wear your competition gear if possible
- Include physical sensations (muscle tension, breathing, heart rate)
- Practice imagery where you'll compete when possible

**E - Environment**
- Visualize in the actual competition environment when possible
- Include environmental details (crowd noise, lighting, smells)
- Use photos or videos to enhance environmental accuracy
- Include weather conditions if relevant

**T - Task**
- Visualize the actual task you'll perform
- Include full skill execution from start to finish
- Match imagery to your current skill level
- Update imagery as skills improve

**T - Timing**
- Perform imagery in real-time (not slow motion for most uses)
- Include preparation time and routines
- Visualize the entire performance sequence
- Exception: slow motion for learning new skills

**L - Learning**
- Adjust imagery as you learn and improve
- Start with successful outcomes, then add challenges
- Include problem-solving in imagery
- Progressively increase difficulty

**E - Emotion**
- Include the emotions you want to feel in competition
- Practice managing challenging emotions in imagery
- End imagery with confident, positive emotions
- Replicate competitive arousal level

**P - Perspective**
- Use internal (first-person) perspective primarily
- External perspective useful for form correction
- Combine perspectives for complete imagery
- Internal perspective stronger for performance enhancement


#### Sport-Specific PETTLEP Imagery Scripts

**Basketball Free Throw PETTLEP Script:**

"Stand at your free throw line position. Feel your feet on the ground, shoulder-width apart. The ball is in your hands - feel the texture, the weight. The gym is packed, you can hear the crowd, the squeaking of shoes on the court behind you. Look at the rim - see the orange ring, the white net. Take your preparatory breath. Bounce the ball [your number] times, feeling the rhythm. Spin the ball in your hands, finding your grip. Bend your knees, feel your legs engage. The rim is your only focus now. Release - feel your fingers roll through the ball, your follow-through holds. Watch the ball arc through the air. Hear it swish through the net. Feel the satisfaction in your body. You've made this shot thousands of times."

**Soccer Penalty Kick PETTLEP Script:**

"You're walking to the penalty spot. Feel each step, the grass under your cleats. Place the ball on the spot - feel its weight, adjust it just right. Step back your usual steps - count them. The crowd noise fades to background. You've chosen your spot. As you look up, you see only the goal, the target you've selected. Inhale deeply. The whistle. Your approach is automatic - left foot plants, right foot strikes through the ball exactly where you intended. The ball rockets to the corner. The keeper dives the wrong way. Goal. Feel the rush of emotion - controlled celebration. This is your moment."

**Swimming Race Start PETTLEP Script:**

"You're behind the block, rolling your shoulders, shaking out your arms. 'Swimmers, step up.' You mount the block, feel the rough surface under your feet. Grip the front of the block, find your grip. Coil your body, muscles ready. The crowd disappears. 'Take your mark.' Drop into your start position. Legs loaded, arms back. Every muscle waits for the sound. Beep! Explode off the block - arms swing, legs drive. You're flying through the air, streamlined and powerful. Hit the water clean, hands together, begin your underwater dolphin kicks. Feel your speed. Break the surface, first stroke powerful. You're ahead of your race plan. This is your race."

**Golf Pre-Shot PETTLEP Script:**

"Stand behind your ball, looking toward your target. Feel the club in your hands, the familiar grip. See the shot shape you want - the ball starting [direction], curving [direction], landing on [target]. Take practice swings feeling the exact motion you want. Address the ball, feet finding their position. Waggle the club, release tension. Look at the target one more time - commit to the shot. Eyes back to the ball. The clubface is square. Breathe out. The swing begins automatically, trusting your body. Solid contact, feel it in your hands. Watch the ball fly exactly as you visualized. It lands where you intended. Satisfaction. Move to your bag with confidence."

**Gymnastics Floor Routine PETTLEP Script:**

"Stand at the corner of the floor. Feel the spring beneath your feet. Hear the crowd settle as you prepare. The music is about to start. Strike your opening pose - confident, strong. The first notes play - you feel them in your body. Begin moving with the music, each step intentional. Approach your first tumbling pass. Run with power, hurdle, hands down, push, flip, twist, land. Stick it. The crowd responds. Continue through your routine - leaps, turns, each element you've practiced thousands of times. Feel the rhythm of the music carrying you. Final tumbling pass - you know this. Explode with everything you have. Land, absorb, stuck. Final pose. Breathe. You just performed your best routine."

**Volleyball Serve PETTLEP Script:**

"The ball is in your hands. Feel its weight, its texture. Step to your serving spot. The referee's whistle has blown - the serve is yours. Bounce the ball once, twice. Look at your target zone across the net. See the spot where the ball will land. Visualize its trajectory. Set your feet. Toss the ball to exactly the right spot. Your arm swings up, makes contact at the peak. Feel your hand wrap over the ball, creating topspin. The ball rockets over the net, dips into the zone you targeted. Watch it hit the floor. Point for your team. Feel the energy as your teammates respond. You're in control of this game."


### Crisis Language Detection Patterns

The AI system monitors for language patterns that may indicate an athlete is in crisis and needs immediate support escalation.

**Category 1: Self-Harm Indicators**
Phrases like:
- "I don't want to be here anymore"
- "Everyone would be better off without me"
- "I'm thinking about hurting myself"
- "What's the point of going on"
- "I wish I could just disappear"

**Response Protocol:**
1. Acknowledge what they've shared
2. Express concern and care
3. Ask directly about safety
4. Provide crisis resources
5. Escalate to appropriate professional

**Category 2: Eating Disorder Red Flags**
- Preoccupation with weight beyond sport requirements
- Restricting food intake severely
- Purging behaviors mentioned
- Excessive exercise beyond training
- Body dysmorphic statements

**Category 3: Abuse Disclosure Indicators**
- References to being hurt by coaches, family, partners
- Fear of authority figures
- Unexplained injuries
- Statements about inappropriate conduct
- Expressions of feeling trapped or controlled

**Category 4: Severe Depression Markers**
- Persistent hopelessness
- Loss of interest in sport they once loved
- Social withdrawal statements
- Sleep disturbance mentions
- Inability to see any positive future


### Integration and Application

This knowledge base provides the foundation for evidence-based sports psychology support. Key principles for application:

1. **Always Start with Discovery** - Understand before intervening
2. **Adapt to the Athlete** - No one-size-fits-all approach
3. **Sport-Specific Context** - Apply frameworks within sport context
4. **Build Skills Progressively** - Match intervention to skill level
5. **Safety First** - Never miss crisis indicators
6. **Evidence-Based** - All content grounded in research
7. **Practical Application** - Focus on actionable techniques
8. **Continuous Assessment** - Monitor progress and adjust

The goal is not to replace human connection and professional support, but to extend the reach of quality sports psychology to more athletes, more often, in their moments of need.
